These are the problems most likely to burn time after a component choice already looks correct on paper.
Review them before wiring production overlays, keyboard navigation, or mixed controlled/uncontrolled state.

## PortalProvider is not optional in serious overlay trees

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Dialog, Popover, Select, Combobox, Menu, Tooltip, and Toast can all render
      without a shared app-level portal target, but layering becomes harder to
      reason about immediately.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Mount one <code>PortalProvider</code> near the app root with a stable{" "}
      <code>PlayerGui</code> container, then let overlay packages inherit it.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Overlay stacking failures usually come from split ownership

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Two overlays can both look correct in isolation but fight once one trigger
      lives under a different provider or display-order base.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Keep one stacking model per app shell. If you need custom layering, do it
      through the shared layer primitives instead of ad hoc ScreenGui trees.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Layering, portals, and presence need one lifecycle

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Portal content can unmount as soon as <code>open</code> flips false, while
      presence or exit motion still expects the subtree to exist. The result is
      a flash, missing exit transition, or stale dismissable layer.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Let <a href="/components/layer/">Layer</a> own mounting and stacking, then
      let <a href="/components/motion/">Motion</a> own the animated phase while
      the content remains mounted through exit completion.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Motion should not fight Popper placement

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Anchored surfaces drift or measure incorrectly when custom animation code
      changes the same position, anchor, or size properties that Popper uses for
      placement.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Keep <a href="/components/popper/">Popper</a> responsible for anchor
      geometry. Animate an isolated appearance or offset wrapper through Motion
      so placement can update without being treated as animation state.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Dialog overlay and panel motion are separate concerns

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      A dialog feels wrong when one transition tries to own backdrop opacity,
      panel reveal, focus trapping, and dismissal at the same time.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Keep <code>Dialog.Overlay</code> and <code>Dialog.Content</code> visually
      independent. Dialog owns interaction semantics; Motion owns the overlay
      fade and panel transition without changing the focus contract.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## forceMount is not an exit-motion switch by itself

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      <code>forceMount</code> keeps a subtree in the React tree, but it does not
      automatically define which phase is visible, exiting, or inert.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Use <code>forceMount</code> for measurement or retained state, then pair it
      with presence-aware behavior when users should see an exit transition.
      Closed content still needs correct visibility and interaction state.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Focus contracts matter more than visuals

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Dialogs and trapped scopes feel flaky when they open from triggers that
      cannot receive focus again or when restore paths are implicit.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Treat the trigger as part of the focus contract. Use{" "}
      <code>restoreFocus</code> intentionally and keep trigger ownership stable
      when possible.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Keyboard flow must survive open, exit, and restore

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Trapped scopes can restore to a missing trigger, move focus into exiting
      content, or let keyboard selection escape while an overlay is still
      visually present.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Treat open state, presence state, and focus scope state as one interaction
      contract. Keep triggers stable, restore focus intentionally, and validate
      keyboard flow in Roblox after preview iteration.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Disabled items still need deliberate product behavior

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Disabled items disappear from interaction, but product state often still
      assumes they can be selected, focused, or announced like normal options.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Model disabled items as truly unavailable. In selection controls, expect
      keyboard and directional movement to skip them and keep labels explicit in
      the rendered host.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Controlled/uncontrolled transitions should be treated as a design choice

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Moving a component between <code>defaultValue</code> and{" "}
      <code>value</code> modes mid-flow creates hard-to-debug state handoff
      issues, especially when the control also owns <code>open</code> or{" "}
      <code>inputValue</code>.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Pick ownership up front. If app state should be the source of truth,
      control the component from the start; otherwise keep it fully uncontrolled
      and mirror only committed changes outward.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>

## Preview is useful, but Roblox runtime is authoritative

<div class="docs-section-grid">
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What breaks</h3>
    <p>
      Loom preview can make an overlay, motion recipe, or focus path look done
      even though Roblox Studio input, selection, timing, or rendering behaves
      differently.
    </p>
  </section>
  <section class="docs-section-card">
    <h3>What to do</h3>
    <p>
      Use preview for fast iteration and screenshots, then manually verify final
      behavior in the Roblox runtime. Preview is not the source of truth for
      shipping keyboard, focus, layering, or motion correctness.
    </p>
  </section>
</div>
